# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two primary profiles, roughly equal in volume:

**Técnico de reparación** (professional): works in a repair shop or independently, buys parts regularly for client jobs. Knows exactly what they need (model, reference, condition), values fast browsing and trustworthy sellers. Often browses on mobile between jobs.

**Reparador aficionado** (amateur): repairs their own device or a family member's. Less confident in identifying the right part, relies on listing detail, condition badges, and direct seller contact. Spain and LATAM, Spanish UI (es-ES locale).

Both profiles also act as sellers when clearing leftover stock or used parts from completed repairs.

## Product Purpose

Fixter is a C2C marketplace for iPhone repair parts (screens, batteries, cameras, connectors, cases, full disassembly units) in Spain and LATAM. It exists to connect the people who have leftover or used parts with the people who need them, at fair prices, with enough trust signals to transact confidently between strangers.

Success: a repair technician finds the exact part they need, contacts the seller, negotiates a price, and completes the transaction — without ever doubting whether the platform is legitimate.

## Brand Personality

Accessible, transparent, precise.

Voice: direct and human, like a knowledgeable colleague. Not corporate, not startup-hype. Explains what something is once, then gets out of the way. Spanish, present tense, no filler.

Emotional goal: the user should feel competent and in control, never confused or pressured.

## Anti-references

- **AliExpress / generic Asian marketplace** — crowded layouts, red price banners, endless promotional badges, low-quality visual hierarchy. Fixter is the opposite: one thing at a time, calm, honest.
- **SaaS startup 2023** — glassmorphism, hero metrics, gradient backgrounds, "supercharge your repair workflow" copy. Fixter is a tool, not a pitch deck.
- **Milanuncios / basic classifieds** — functional but visually uncared. If the interface looks like 2009, trust signals that cost us real effort (reviews, condition badges, verified profiles) will be dismissed as equally outdated.
- **Amazon / MediaMarkt** — corporate and cold. No sense of community or human transaction. Fixter is peer-to-peer; the seller is a person, not a warehouse.

## Design Principles

1. **The interface disappears into the transaction.** Find → Evaluate → Contact → Agree. Every screen has one primary action. Remove anything that slows down that path.
2. **Trust is visual, not textual.** Condition badges, review scores, seller metadata, and image quality are first-class layout citizens, not footnotes. A user deciding whether to buy should never have to hunt for the signals that let them decide.
3. **Technical precision, human tone.** Repair technicians use jargon (LCD, OLED, batería original, flex cable). The interface mirrors that vocabulary without becoming cold. The tone is that of a knowledgeable colleague.
4. **Mobile-first, density when needed.** Many users browse between jobs on a phone. Forms and cards are thumb-reachable. On desktop, take advantage of space for side-by-side layouts and denser data where appropriate.
5. **Consistency earns confidence.** Same button shape, same icon style, same form vocabulary everywhere. Inconsistency in a marketplace signals that the operator doesn't care — and if they don't care, why would a seller or buyer trust the transaction?

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA. Minimum contrast 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text and UI components. Keyboard-navigable primary flows (browse, publish, contact seller, send message). Reduced motion alternatives for all transitions. Screen reader labels on icon-only buttons and form fields.
